What is Vaping?

Vaping involves using an electronic device, often called an e-cigarette or vape, to heat a liquid (e-liquid or vape juice) until it turns into an aerosol (often mistakenly called vapour) that is then inhaled. These devices come in many shapes and sizes, from sleek pens to larger "mods," and are often marketed with appealing designs and flavours.

It's crucial to understand that what you inhale from a vape is NOT just harmless water vapour. E-liquids typically contain a mix of chemicals, including:

  • Propylene Glycol (PG) & Vegetable Glycerine (VG): These are the main ingredients that create the aerosol. While generally safe for ingestion, their effects when heated and inhaled deep into the lungs are still being studied and are a cause for concern.
  • Nicotine: A highly addictive chemical found naturally in tobacco. Many vapes, even some marketed as "nicotine-free," contain nicotine.
  • Flavourings: Thousands of different flavourings are used, from fruit and candy to dessert flavours. Many of these chemicals are safe to eat but not safe to inhale, and can break down into harmful substances when heated.
  • Other Chemicals: Vapes can contain other harmful chemicals, heavy metals (like lead, tin, and nickel from the heating coil), and ultrafine particles that can be inhaled deep into the lungs.

The Serious Dangers of Vaping

1. Nicotine Addiction & Brain Harm

Nicotine is a highly addictive chemical, and the adolescent brain is especially vulnerable. Your brain continues to develop until around age 25, and nicotine exposure during this critical period can cause lasting negative effects.

  • Cognitive Impairment: Harms areas of the brain involved in attention, learning, and memory.
  • Mood Regulation: Can negatively impact emotional control and lead to mood swings.
  • Impulse Control: Affects your ability to make rational decisions.
  • "Priming" for Addiction: Nicotine can make your brain more susceptible to addiction to other substances later in life.
  • Rapid Addiction: In South Africa, a study showed that 47% of teen vapers use their device within the first hour of waking, a strong sign of high nicotine addiction.

2. Severe Physical Health Risks

Despite marketing claims, vaping introduces harmful chemicals into your body, leading to a range of serious physical health problems.

  • Lung Damage: Can cause "popcorn lung" (bronchiolitis obliterans), severe inflammation, and acute lung injuries, some of which have been fatal. Vaping affects 358 genes involved in fighting viruses and bacteria, compared to 53 for cigarettes.
  • Heart Health: Nicotine speeds up heart rate and raises blood pressure. Chemicals in vape aerosol can damage blood vessels, increasing the risk of heart attacks and strokes.
  • Weakened Immune System: Vaping can suppress the lungs' natural defenses, making you more vulnerable to infections like colds, flu, and pneumonia.
  • Exposure to Toxins: You inhale formaldehyde, heavy metals (lead, tin, nickel), and ultrafine particles. Flavouring chemicals like diacetyl are linked to serious lung disease. Some chemicals are found in cleaning products, nail polish remover, and weed killer.
  • Oral Health: Leads to dry mouth, increased risk of gingivitis and periodontitis (gum disease), and other irreversible damage to your mouth and gums.
  • Skin Issues: Propylene glycol can dehydrate your skin, and nicotine restricts blood flow, leading to dryness and premature aging/sagging.
  • Explosions: Though rare, faulty vape batteries have been known to explode, causing severe burns and injuries.

3. Impact on Mental Health

The link between vaping and mental health is significant and concerning, especially for young people.

  • Increased Anxiety & Depression: Vaping can worsen symptoms of pre-existing mental health conditions and increase the incidence of new cases of depression and anxiety.
  • Coping Mechanism: Many teens start vaping to cope with stress and anxiety, but it often exacerbates these issues rather than resolving them.
  • Suicidal Thoughts: Studies show a higher incidence of depression, suicidal thoughts, and suicide attempts in teenagers who vape compared to those who don't.

4. Gateway to Traditional Smoking

Vaping is often marketed as a safer alternative or a way to quit smoking, but for young people, it often acts as a gateway to traditional cigarette use.

  • Global studies show that teens who vape are significantly more likely to start smoking conventional cigarettes within months.
  • In South Africa, one in five (19%) users with no previous smoking history started smoking conventional cigarettes only after using e-cigarettes.
  • The appealing flavours and sleek designs of vapes are specifically designed to attract young consumers, creating a new generation of nicotine-dependent individuals.

5. Second-hand and Third-hand Exposure

Even if you don't vape, you can still be exposed to harmful substances from others' vaping.

  • Second-hand Aerosol: The exhaled "vape cloud" contains nicotine and tiny harmful particles that can affect people nearby, especially children, pregnant women, and those with respiratory conditions like asthma.
  • Third-hand Exposure: Residual nicotine and other chemicals from vape aerosol can settle on indoor surfaces (furniture, clothes, walls) long after the "cloud" has cleared. This can lead to exposure through skin contact, inhalation, or ingestion, particularly risky for young children.

Vaping in South Africa: What You Need to Know

Concerning Statistics Among SA Youth

Vaping is a growing crisis among South African teenagers, with alarming rates of use and addiction:

  • A 2023 study across 52 high schools found that 17.82% of learners (Grades 8-12) currently use vaping products, and 36.71% have tried vaping.
  • The prevalence increases with age: 9% of Grade 8 learners vape, rising to an average of 30% among Matric learners (some schools seeing rates as high as 46%).
  • 38% of students vape daily, with an additional 50% vaping at least four days a week.
  • 12% of learners reported being unable to get through the school day without vaping, highlighting severe dependence.
  • Over 50% of students cited social influences (friends, peer pressure) as reasons for starting, while nearly 20% began vaping to cope with stress and anxiety.
  • Vape shops are often located near tertiary education institutions, making them easily accessible.

South African Laws & Proposed Regulations

Currently, South Africa's vaping regulation is largely governed by the outdated Tobacco Products Control Act (2008), which doesn't fully address e-cigarettes. However, there are ongoing efforts to introduce stricter laws through the Tobacco Products and Electronic Delivery Systems Control Bill.

While this Bill is still being finalised, some aspects, like age restrictions, are already being enforced.

  • Age Restrictions: The sale of e-cigarettes and vaping devices to individuals under the age of 18 is strictly prohibited.
  • Public Use Bans: Smoking and vaping are banned in all workplaces, indoor public spaces, and public transport.
  • Proposed Restrictions (from the Bill):
    • Bans on certain e-cigarette products, including flavoured and disposable vapes.
    • Increased taxes on vaping products to make them less affordable.
    • Strict advertising and promotion bans (e.g., no sponsorship of events, no social media marketing).
    • Mandatory explicit health warnings and nicotine content information on packaging.
    • Products may not be displayed at point of sale; retailers can only make them available upon request (and only to non-minors).
  • Penalties for Non-Compliance (Proposed): The Bill outlines significant financial penalties, potential imprisonment (up to 3 months for minor offenses, up to 20 years for severe offenses), business closure, and product confiscation for individuals and businesses violating the regulations.
